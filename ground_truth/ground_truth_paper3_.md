comment accepted June 2026

I have examined the data presented in this paper and reviewed the authors’ response. I offer the following observations in response.

**Point 1 – last five decimals in BV/TV (Fig 2e & Fig 7e)**

The authors attribute the identical decimal endings to manual measurement, Excel retaining nine decimal places, and truncation to six decimals. They provide the raw pre truncation values as 5.438082683 and 5.838083015. Truncation to six decimals would give 5.438082 and 5.838083, whose last five digits (38082 and 38083) are not identical. However, the published values are 5.438083 and 5.838083, and their last five digits (38083) are identical. This pattern is consistent with rounding, not truncation, so the explanation does not account for the published numbers. Furthermore, the two experiments involved completely independent animal cohorts – a gene knockout model (Fig 2e) and an ovariectomy induced osteoporosis model (Fig 7e) – with separate housing, tissue processing, sectioning, and manual measurements. Under such independent conditions, matching of the last five decimal digits across the two datasets would be highly unexpected.

**Point 2 – three identical Nron values in Suppl Fig 5a**

The authors state that two rounds of normalization produced the three identical values. In qPCR analysis, normalization is applied separately to each independent experiment, typically by scaling to a reference sample. This rescaling does not eliminate the inherent biological and technical variation among independent replicates. If the raw Ct values from three separate experiments differed, the normalized values would still reflect those differences. Obtaining exactly the same normalized value from three independent experiments would require that the raw values also be essentially identical – an outcome not typical for biological replicates. The raw Ct values and unnormalized expression data for each experiment should therefore be provided.

**Point 3 – repeated numbers within and between groups**

The authors offer several explanations for the repeated numbers. For Ct.Th and MAR, they state that identical averages resulted from averaging multiple measuring points. For the apoptosis percentage, they note that two samples happened to have 22 total cells and 3 dead cells, leading to the same calculated percentage. For Tb.N and Tb.Th, they attribute repeated values to limited instrument resolution and unified scanning parameters. Each explanation might seem plausible on its own. However, when all repeated numbers are considered together – across averages, cell counts, and micro CT parameters – it becomes difficult to attribute every instance to these separate reasons. For example, if instrument resolution is so limited that values repeat, reporting them to four or five decimal places becomes inconsistent with that limited resolution. Moreover, several independent mice having exactly the same average cortical thickness, or two samples having the exact same total and dead cell counts, would represent highly improbable coincidences.

**Point 4 – same decimal parts with different integers**

The authors explain that division with a fixed denominator (e.g., 229.333 in Suppl Fig 5c) can produce results in which the decimal parts are identical even when the integer parts differ. Although mathematically sound, this explanation overlooks the inherent variability of the numerators. The numerators in these calculations derive from actual biological measurements (cell counts, etc.), which contain random variation. Even with a fixed denominator, small fluctuations in the numerator would alter the decimal part of the result. For the decimal parts to be identical across multiple independent samples, the numerators would need to vary in a highly specific, non random way – for instance, differing by exact multiples of the denominator.

**Point 5 – fixed constant arithmetic rules**

The authors point to identical denominators and systematic error from the μCT instrument to explain the precise multiplicative and additive relationships. Each explanation may be plausible in isolation. However, the presence of both a 1.5× and a 2.25× relationship in the same figure, along with constant offsets that differ from one comparison to another – some positive, some negative – makes the overall pattern harder to reconcile with routine measurement behavior. In measurement science, systematic error typically affects all measurements in a batch in the same direction and by approximately the same magnitude. It would not yield different constant offsets for different group comparisons, as observed here.

The authors’ response cites procedural details such as truncation, normalization, averaging, denominator relationships, and systematic error. However, a close examination shows that these explanations either do not fit the published numbers (truncation versus rounding) or rely on assumptions that are inconsistent with typical biological variation and measurement theory.

Regarding Point 1, the authors state that the matching decimal endings result from rounding. The authors have provided raw nine‑decimal values that, when rounded, yield the published matching sequences. Under the assumption of a uniform distribution of the decimal digits, the probability that two independent measurements, when rounded to six decimal places, have identical digits in positions 2 through 6 (five digits) is 1 in 100,000.
